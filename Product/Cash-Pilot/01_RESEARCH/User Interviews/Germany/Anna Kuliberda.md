# Anna Kuliberda

Business: Freelance coach/facilitator
Conclusion: The interview with Anna Kuliberda strongly confirms the need for a cash flow forecasting feature, but highlights that its success will be driven more by trust and psychological safety than raw complexity.The most critical design imperatives for this feature are:

1. Prioritize Trust and Safety: Mitigate the user's financial anxiety with an intuitive, low-text interface, positive reinforcement notifications ("you're doing well"), and absolute accuracy in analyzing past data.

2. Focus on Actionable Patterns: Users like Anna will prioritize seeing reliable, contextual patterns and trends over precise, potentially misleading future numbers. The suggested one-year forecast should emphasize this pattern-based visualization.

3. Deliver High-Impact Features: The key functionalities requested are a "What If" simulation mode for planning decisions like pension contributions, and specific, high-value alerts for tax compliance (e.g., the German "fake employment" 80% rule) and tracking recurring annual expenses/subscriptions.

4. Emphasize Clear Communication: The feature must "outsource" administrative effort from the user's head and use clear, supportive language to explain accounting concepts, thereby building credibility where other financial apps have failed.

Anna Kuliberda, a self-employed professional, regarding the development of a cash flow forecasting feature for an accounting SaaS product.Interview Summary: Insights for Cash Flow Forecasting Feature1. User Profile & Business Context

- **User Segment:** Small business owner, self-employed coach/consultant/educator working primarily with non-profit organizations.
- **Administrative Burden:** Handles day-to-day administration and cash flow monitoring herself, relying on a spreadsheet and AirTable, as her current accounting setup (Contis bank, Wise for non-Euro currencies, and a German accountant) requires her to bridge the gaps in oversight.
- **Financial Complexity:** Operates internationally, dealing with multiple currencies and complex German tax requirements (specifically, the "fake employment" rule regarding 80% income from one client).
- **Cash Flow Monitoring:** Monitors cash flow monthly (when calm), bi-weekly (when busy with business travel/short-term activity), and quarterly (for long-term planning).

2. Primary Pain Points

- **Lack of Clarity & Automation:** Spends excessive manual effort trying to keep track of real-time expenses, recurring subscriptions, and patterns in her head. She desires to "outsource it from the back of my head to the front of my eyes."
- **Anxiety & Trust:** Describes herself as an "anxious, control freak" when it comes to finances. She is wary of her current bank's communication, which is often perceived as negative or alarming ("hey, we need to talk"). Trust is a major barrier to adopting new tools, requiring data accuracy, especially for past financial analysis.
- **Actionable Advice Gap:** Struggles with long-term financial decisions, like when and how much to contribute to a personal pension account, and needs features that provide data-driven "incentives" and "positive reinforcement."

3. Key Feature Requirements & Design Priorities

The user’s requests are split between core functionality, forecasting accuracy, and psychological experience.

| Category | Key Requirements |
| --- | --- |
| **Core Functionality** | **Accurate Historical Analysis:** Must analyze past data correctly to identify patterns, recurring events (like big annual clients), and distinguish them from one-time events ("clean the noise"). |
|  | **Subscription/Expense Tracking:** Automate tracking of annual and recurring subscriptions/expenses (e.g., alert for December's high expenses). |
|  | **Single Account Focus:** One main account connection (Contis) is sufficient, as she handles currency conversion (Wise) manually. |
| **Forecasting & Insight** | **One-Year Forecast:** A one-year projection is the preferred date range. |
|  | **Pattern Focus:** Prioritizes seeing reliable **patterns** and **trends** over precise, potentially misleading future numbers ("I would not trust numbers"). |
|  | **Simulation Mode (What-If):** Must-have feature for scenario planning, like simulating the impact of setting aside a percentage for a pension. |
| **User Experience (UX) & Trust** | **Intuitive & Low-Text:** The interface should be intuitive and self-explanatory, with minimal text, per the product designer’s intent. |
|  | **Clear Communication:** **The language used to explain concepts is paramount.** She wants context to understand accounting terms, not "fluff" or redundant, complex language. |
|  | **Positive Reinforcement System:** Notifications should mitigate anxiety and provide positive coaching/grounding (e.g., "Good job, you're doing well," or a notification that she can comfortably afford a pension contribution). |
|  | **Compliance Alert:** A specific, urgent alert mechanism for the **German 80% "fake employment" rule** is required for balancing client income. |
| **Platform Preference** | **Desktop-First:** Prefers using the tool on a desktop for better context and understanding. |---
