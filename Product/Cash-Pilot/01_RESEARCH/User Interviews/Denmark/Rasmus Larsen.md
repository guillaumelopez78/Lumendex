# Rasmus Larsen

Business: Small farm
Conclusion: A user who values manual control and simplicity over automation. For very small businesses like Rasmus's, the primary barrier to adopting new fintech tools is the perceived lack of necessity and a distrust of "black box" automated systems.
Follow ups: No
Interview Date: 6 février 2026
Meeting notes: https://docs.google.com/document/d/1kMMuKJdSfwFuZsJSsjYWfh3o1-t3sNrdLiIRsPaxJAs/edit?tab=t.3n1m8fcok9s1
Pain points: Rasmus’s pain points stem from a deep distrust of automation and a feeling that modern tools are designed for businesses much larger than his.
• 
Distrust of "Artificial Stupidity": He feels that automated systems often miscategorize data, making it harder to find information than if he had handled it manually.

• 
Irrelevant Product Categorization: He is frustrated that the current system often defaults to categories like "freelance photographer" for his farm, making the tool feel like it wasn't built for him.

• 
Lack of Granularity in Visuals: He finds existing graphs "annoying" if he cannot click on a specific data point (like a green income bar) to see exactly which sale (e.g., meat or eggs) it represents.

• 
Forced Complexity: He feels burdened by "upgrades" or new features that he cannot turn off, preferring to keep his management limited to what he can "overlook" in his head or bank account.

• 
Tax Adjustment Friction: He recently found an upgrade meant to adjust taxes "fucking annoying," suggesting that even helpful features feel like a hindrance if the interface is not seamless.

This user interview features Martin Albrecht Kvien (interviewer) and Rasmus Larsen (user), a small business owner. The conversation centers on Rasmus's skepticism regarding automated accounting tools and liquidity forecasting for his specific business needs.
Interview Summary
User Profile & Business Context
•
Company Type: Rasmus describes his business as a "little hobby business" or a small farm with animals.

- Size: He has no employees and currently does not take a salary, reinvesting all income back into the business.
- Financial Philosophy: He prefers paying cash for investments rather than borrowing money.

Current Financial Management
•
Liquidity Tracking: Rasmus tracks liquidity simply by monitoring his bank account. He feels his business is small enough that he can "overlook" the finances without complicated tools.

- Tools Used: He uses a manual cash book to track currency outflows. He does not currently use an automated accounting tool, though he has an accountant who prepares his annual reports.

Key Concerns & Skepticism
•
Automation & AI: Rasmus is highly skeptical of "automated processes" and "artificial intelligence," which he jokingly refers to as "artificial stupidity".

- Complexity: He fears that automation makes running a business more difficult and less transparent. He is concerned that automated systems might miscategorize data, making it harder to find or understand.
- Value Proposition: He questions the utility of a liquidity prognosis tool for his scale, stating, "What the hell should I use it for, when I now have my own bank account".

Product Feedback & Opportunities
•
Desire for Granularity: While skeptical of graphs, Rasmus noted that if he were to use such a system, he would need to be able to click on visual data (like red or green bars) to see the specific background information (e.g., selling meat or eggs).

- Feature Control: He expressed a strong desire to be able to turn off automated features or remove them if they are not useful to him.
- Interest in Detail: He eventually admitted that a system where he could "click on everything" to see detailed information is something he "could use".

Conclusion
The interview reveals a user who values manual control and simplicity over automation. For very small businesses like Rasmus's, the primary barrier to adopting new fintech tools is the perceived lack of necessity and a distrust of "black box" automated systems.---
