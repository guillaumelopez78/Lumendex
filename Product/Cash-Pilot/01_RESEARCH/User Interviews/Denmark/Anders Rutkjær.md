# Anders Rutkjær

Business: Carpentry w. several employees
Conclusion: Anders’ approach to liquidity is currently reactive and manual, making him an ideal candidate for an automated forecasting tool.

Key Takeaways from the Interview

• Manual Overload: Anders manages liquidity "in his head" by checking bank balances against mental tallies of upcoming obligations.

• Seasonal Vulnerability: With a monthly burn rate of 300,000 DKK, he needs a tool to ensure high-season profits sufficiently cover the winter low-season.

• Predictability Gaps: He often forgets significant recurring costs, such as a 100,000 DKK insurance bill due every January 1st.

• Desired Visibility: He requires a 3-to-12-month visual runway that accounts for Moms (VAT), holiday money debt, and tax obligations.
Follow ups: Yes
Interview Date: 24 février 2026
Pain points: Anders' pain points regarding cash flow forecasting:

• Manual "Mental" Tracking: He relies on his head or a blog to track numbers , forcing him to manually compare bank balances against upcoming bills.

• Seasonal "Dead Periods": He struggles to predict if high-season earnings will cover a monthly burn rate of 300,000 DKK during the winter low season.

• Invisible Large Expenses: He often forgets major annual costs, like a 100,000 DKK insurance bill on January 1st, until they hit his account.

• Opaque Tax/Legal Liabilities: It is difficult for him to track exactly how much cash is reserved for Moms (VAT) , holiday money , and tax debt.

• Runway Uncertainty: He lacks a clear visual of his "runway"—specifically knowing if current trends mean he will run out of money in 6, 12, or 18 months.
