Inconsistent Debtor Payments: He finds it difficult to manage liquidity when customers do not pay on time or when payments are pulled out over long periods.

This interview features Martin Albrecht Kvien (interviewer) and Pilu Kasper Bech (user), who runs a small bookkeeping business. The discussion explores Pilu's professional needs and those of her clients regarding a new liquidity prognosis feature being developed for the Billy accounting platform.

Interview Summary
User Profile & Business Context
• Business: A one-year-old small bookkeeping firm that assists various clients, including many small craftsmen.

- Role: Pilu is the founder and director.
- Turnover: Approximately 200,000 DKK last year.

Current Liquidity Management
•
Methods: She primarily monitors liquidity by checking her bank account and tracking money going in and out.

- Frequency: She reviews finances roughly once a week, depending on the current state of liquidity.
- Challenges: Her main difficulty is managing "debtors" (clients who do not pay on time) and tracking upcoming falling bills.

Feedback on Liquidity Prognosis Feature
•
Desired Forecast Period: Pilu initially focuses on the immediate future (next week to 14 days) but agrees that a long-term view (six months to a year) would also be valuable.

- Simulation & "What-If" Scenarios: She is highly interested in a tool that allows for simulations—such as seeing how a major investment (like a new car) or a specific upcoming invoice would impact her future cash balance.
- Notifications: She finds the idea of alerts for liquidity limits or expected surpluses "abstract" but useful for planning investments.
- Platform Preference: She strongly prefers a web/computer version over mobile. She views the phone as a tool for quick tasks like scanning receipts, while the web version allows for deeper analysis and "playing" with different factors on a larger screen.

Business Practicalities
•
Pricing: Pilu suggested a price point of around 25 DKK per month as reasonable, provided the tool works effectively and makes sense for her business.

- Primary Use Cases: She would use the tool for general financial oversight, loan applications, and planning major purchases or investments like warehouse rent for her clients.

Conclusion
Pilu represents a dual-value user: she is interested in the tool for her own operations and as a value-add service for her bookkeeping clients. Her feedback emphasizes that for a liquidity tool to be successful, it must move beyond static data and allow for dynamic simulation.

The core takeaway is that while high-level graphs are nice, the true "pain point" she wants to solve is uncertainty. A tool that allows her to "type in data" to see future impacts —especially regarding large investments or unreliable payments—is what would drive her to pay for the service. Her strong preference for the web platform suggests that the development team should prioritize the full-screen analytical experience over a simplified mobile app.---
