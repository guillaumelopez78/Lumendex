# Herke Oudeboon

Business: Financial risk manager
Conclusion: The interview confirms that a cash flow forecast tool is conceptually interesting for small business owners, but its utility hinges on accuracy and user control.

The key takeaways are:

• Reliability is paramount: The tool must first provide a solid assessment of past cash flow to build user trust, as an unrealistic forecast will be ignored.

• Flexibility for Freelancers: Users with irregular income (like freelancers) must be able to easily edit and override the app's income assumptions.

• Actionable Insights: The most valuable features are automated detection of irregular payments and missed bills, rather than complex financial KPIs.

• Data Accuracy: Core issues like poor expense categorization and failure to account for planned payment dates must be solved for the forecast to be precise for the crucial short-term (3-month) horizon.

Pain points: • Inaccurate Expense Categorization: The app often fails to correctly identify recurring suppliers and defaults to "general costs," making the forecast unreliable.

• Unreliable Income Forecasting: The app cannot accurately predict irregular, project-based income for freelancers, making the default forecast unhelpful.

• Lack of Planned Payment Timing: The forecast is inaccurate because it uses invoice due dates rather than the user's planned payment dates, which is critical for short-term cash flow.

• Need for Verification: Users will not trust the forecast unless it is grounded in and consistent with their past cash flow data.

This interview with Herke Oudeboon, a freelancer and trained financial risk manager, provides several key insights, pain points, and potential feature suggestions for the development of a cash flow forecast app for small business owners.Key Pain Points and Feature Needs for the Cash Flow Forecast App

| Category | Pain Point/Issue | Suggested Feature/Solution |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Categorization/Recurring Payments** | The current system often fails to correctly recognize recurring suppliers and expense categories, frequently defaulting to "general costs." This is a significant issue for accurate forecasting. | **Improved Machine Learning/AI:** Better models are needed to automatically and correctly recognize regular suppliers and categorize bills into the right expense rubrics (e.g., internet, insurance, rent). Users must be able to recognize their regular costs in the forecast. |
| **Income Forecasting** | Herke, as a freelancer, has irregular, project-based income that the app cannot reliably predict long-term. An automated prediction is not useful if it's unrealistic. | **User-Editable Forecasts:** Users must be able to change the app's income assumptions, especially for irregular income (e.g., adding or adjusting project income, defining project end dates). |
| **Past Data Verification** | A forecast that doesn't align with the user's past data or history will be considered unreliable and ignored. | **Regular Assessment of Past Cash Flow:** The app should first provide a clear assessment of past cash flow (income statement, balance statement, cash flow statement) so the user can "recognize the past and feel comfortable with it" before trusting the forecast. |
| **Timing of Payments** | The current system gathers due dates from invoices but doesn't account for when the user *plans* to make a payment, which can be weeks later, impacting the cash position. | **Planned Payment Integration:** The app needs to know the user's *planned* payment dates, ideally through a connection with the bank's future payment data, for the short-term forecast to be precise. |
| **Engagement/Format** | Herke views financial reporting (including the forecast) as "slow thinking" that requires a computer. However, quick alerts would be helpful on mobile. | **Dual Interface:** Full reports and in-depth views should be optimized for a desktop/laptop. Urgent signals and notifications, which prompt action, are best suited for a mobile app. |

High-Value Actions and Signals

Herke was more interested in

**signals**

and

**actionable insights**

than complex Financial KPIs (like margin or cost-to-income ratio).

- **Irregularity/Anomaly Detection:** The app should flag payments that are "much higher than we've seen in the past" or "does not fit your pattern of cost or income" to prompt a user check.
- **Missed Payment Reminders:** The system should alert the user if an expected recurring payment (rent, VAT, etc.) has not been seen in the banking transactions after the due date has passed.
- **Automated Savings/Investment:** A very helpful feature would be a "cash management" tool that automates saving: if the cash balance is above a certain level and no payments are due, the app could automatically transfer the surplus to a separate savings account (within the app's banking feature).
- **Pension Reminders/Integration:** Especially for small entrepreneurs in the Netherlands, the app could be used to remind them of the need to build up their own pension and potentially integrate with local pension product partners.

Other Contextual Details

- **Forecast Horizon:** Herke's preferred forecast length is **three months**, with a maximum of a year.
- **Primary KPI:** The **cash position** is the most important output of the forecast, as "cash is different from income."
- **User Profile (Herke):** He is a small business owner, a freelancer, is financially literate, and manages his own bookkeeping. He is not "cash-constrained" and has a low volume of transactions (trying to stay under 20 per month to avoid fees), meaning the app's value for him is more "nice to have" rather than a "must have" that he would pay a high price for.---
