# Harry Schutte

Business: Accountant
Conclusion: The core conclusion is that the new cash flow forecasting feature must address two main objectives to fill a market gap:

1. Dual Focus on Forecasting: The tool must seamlessly integrate both:
    ◦ Short-Term Liquidity: For crisis management (weekly/monthly checks on bank/invoice data) with proactive alerts.
    ◦ Long-Term Strategy: For strategic planning (3-5 years) with a flexible simulation mode (what-if scenarios).

2. Overcoming User Adoption Barriers: The app needs to be highly intuitive and simplify complex finance by providing a "translation of the figures." It must clearly demonstrate value to non-financial users and offer flexibility to compete with custom Excel models.
Follow ups: Yes
Interview Date: 13 février 2026
Pain points: 1. Process & Functionality

• Lack of Future Payment Planning
His current banking/bookkeeping system (Swan) is very "flat" and does not allow for planning payments in the future (e.g., for 14 or 15 days out), forcing immediate execution of payments.

• Complexity of Reporting
With the current simple system (T), preparing annual reports is cumbersome, requiring him to export data twice to Excel, manually combine it, and then import it to the report system.

• Manual & Labor-Intensive Forecasting
Harry has to build his own cash flow forecast models using Excel, which is time-consuming and requires significant manual work to adjust for each new client's specific variables and needs.

2. Market & Product

• Lack of Integrated Forecasting Tools
There are very few financially-based forecast programs available on the market that are flexible, easy to use, and can effectively translate figures for the average non-financial user.

3. Client Experience

• Short-Term Liquidity Crises Management
When clients face severe cash flow problems, they are "in panic." Harry's initial job is often to spend time calming them down before they can start working on solutions to their liquidity shortage.

4. Client Behavior & Adoption

• Lack of Financial Interest/Understanding
Many clients (who are commercial or technical entrepreneurs) are not genuinely interested in financial figures or annual reports and do not see the value in accurate bookkeeping or forecasting tools, making adoption difficult.

• Resistance to New Tools (The "Excel Barrier")
Users who have built their own detailed forecasts in Excel are often "very proud" of their custom models. They view a new commercial software with fixed limits on flexibility as a barrier to their preferred, customizable way of working.

- Role: Owner of a small accountancy company, working with his wife. He handles annual reports and tax forms, while his wife manages bookkeeping and salaries.
- Clients: Approximately 25 business clients and 50 private clients, for whom they provide tax form and annual report support.
- Experience: Has been operating his company for 35-40 years, witnessing the evolution of bookkeeping from manual to computerized systems.

Current Cash Flow Forecasting Method

- Tool: Harry primarily uses Excel to build custom models for his clients' forecasts.
- Basis: Forecasts are based on the client's previous year's annual reports, adjusted for expected changes like growth (by percentage or volume).
- Forecast Periods:
◦ Mid-term/Long-term: Standardly three years, with an option for up to five years, based on annual reports and profit/loss.
◦ Short-term (Liquidity Focus): For clients with severe cash flow problems, a forecast is needed for the next weeks or a month, sometimes even checked daily or weekly. This short-term forecast is strictly based on liquidity (bank accounts, outstanding invoices, payments due) and not profit/loss.

Key Requirements and Insights for the New Tool

- Simulation Mode ("What-If" Scenarios): Harry is very interested in a "simulation mode" where users can test different scenarios (e.g., stopping an activity, changes in payment timings) to see the impact on liquidity and profit/loss.
- Alert/Notification System: An alert system would be valuable, especially for short-term liquidity issues, notifying users when they are approaching a critical low level (e.g., a specific bank limit).
- Flexibility and Levels of Forecast: The tool should offer flexibility to forecast:
1. Short-Term Liquidity: Based on data already in the bookkeeping system (invoices, payments).
2. Mid/Long-Term P&L and Balance: Based on annual reports and making assumptions for future incoming data.
3. Advanced Inputs: Ability to factor in seasonal influences, different products with different turnover percentages, and learning from past payment behavior of specific clients (e.g., late-paying clients).
- Interface: The software should be able to connect to different bookkeeping software programs, with an interface that is as universal as possible (e.g., using Excel exports).

Barriers to User Adoption

- Convincing Non-Financial Users: The biggest barrier is convincing non-financial users (commercial or technical entrepreneurs) of the tool's value, as they are not typically interested in figures. The software needs to "explain the figures" and offer storytelling to build trust and show how it can help them make critical business decisions.
- Cost: A reasonable monthly subscription price is more acceptable than a high one-time cost.
- Competition from Custom Tools: Users who have built their own flexible Excel forecasts may be resistant to a new tool, viewing their own solution as the best. The new feature needs to be highly flexible to compete.---
